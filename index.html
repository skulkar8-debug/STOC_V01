  • ~80–81% operate as single‑location practices.[web:7][web:26]

3. Private equity momentum

Why medspas fit the PE playbook
  • Cash‑pay revenue model with limited reimbursement risk and strong consumer willingness to pay for outcomes and experience.[web:10][web:31][web:33]
  • Recurring and subscription‑like revenue from injectables, skin maintenance and memberships, supporting predictable cohort economics.[web:10][web:31][web:33]
  • Unit‑driven growth: relatively modest capex per site, replicable clinical/operating model and clear levers across pricing, utilization and cross‑sell.[web:26][web:31][web:33]

5. Data gaps in current medspa investing

Fragmented information, limited comparability
  • Location lists are incomplete and inconsistent, making it difficult to build a comprehensive, deduplicated map of the investable medspa universe.[web:7][web:21][web:26]
  • Digital presence (websites, social media, reviews) varies widely across clinics; without structured scraping and scoring, these signals remain qualitative and non‑comparable.
  • Staffing and clinical oversight data (MDs, NPs, injectors) are scattered across state registries, clinic bios and third‑party platforms, and rarely aggregated pre‑LOI.

8. Risk, scenarios and underwriting discipline

Key risk factors
  • Regulatory and scope‑of‑practice: Variation in supervision requirements and delegation rules by state; risk of enforcement tightening.[web:7][web:10]
  • Talent and staffing: Competition for experienced injectors and medical directors; wage inflation and turnover affecting service capacity.[web:30][web:33]
  • Macro and discretionary spend: Sensitivity to consumer confidence and interest rates, though recent periods show surprising resilience.[web:10][web:31]
